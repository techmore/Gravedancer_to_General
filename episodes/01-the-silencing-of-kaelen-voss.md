## DAY 1: The First Rain

The rain on Kalee did not fall so much as *arrive* — heavy, deliberate, and
full of memory. Qymaen jai Sheelal stood at the lip of the gorge and let it
strike his mask, each drop a small percussion against the bone-white lacquer
the Kaleesh painted for war.

Below, in the black mud of the Jiwari lowlands, a Republic shuttle had come
down wrong. Not a crash — a landing that *pretended* to be a crash, the kind
of theater the offworlders favored when they wanted someone found without
admitting they were looking.

He could smell the ozone of the hull still bleeding heat into the wet earth.
And under it, faint as a confession: the clean, mineral scent that clung to
those who carried lightsabers.

"One," he said, to no one. The word was a count. The word was a promise.

His lieutenant, Rolk, did not like it. Rolk liked few things that bled
plasma and carried the Republic's quiet blessings.

"They send a single one for the gravesinger?" Rolk asked, crouched beside
him in the lee of a basalt shelf. "That is either an insult or a trap."

"It is a *test*," Qymaen said. He did not look away from the gorge. "They
wish to learn what we are, before they decide what we are worth."

"And what are we?"

Qymaen smiled behind the mask. It was not a kind expression. "We are the
answer to a question they have not yet been brave enough to ask."

The shuttle's ramp lowered. A figure descended — green-skinned, slight,
moving with the careful economy of someone who had been taught that the
ground itself might be an enemy. A Mirialan. The tattoos on her face were
geometric, precise, the kind of marking that meant a counting of sins.

Kaelen Voss. The name had reached him three systems away, carried by a
Huk trader who thought he was buying his life with information. He had not.

Qymaen watched her step off the metal and onto Kalee, and he felt the
Gravedancer stir in the back of his skull — that cold, patient thing that
counted the living and found them wanting.

*So it begins*, he thought. And the rain kept its rhythm, and did not
answer.

## DAY 2: The Counting of Steps

She did not go where the Republic expected her to go.

Qymaen had laid his ambush at the wreckage — three rifles on the high
ground, a spike-pit covered in flood debris, and himself with the long
blade his father hadforged from the spine of a Huk war-machine. Standard.
Clean. The kind of trap that worked on soldiers.

Kaelen Voss was not a soldier.

She paused two hundred meters short of the wreckage, knelt in the mud
without any apparent regard for her robes, and placed both palms flat on
the earth. Qymaen watched through the magnifier as her eyes closed and her
breathing slowed to something barely human.

*She is listening to us*, he realized. Not with her ears. With the other
thing — the thing the Jedi carried like a second heart, the thing that
made them so dangerous and so, in the end, so very predictable.

He touched Rolk's shoulder. Two taps. *Hold.*

"We kill her now," Rolk breathed. "While she kneels."

"No."

"She is *reading us* —"

"I know." Qymaen's voice was steady, almost tender. "That is what I want
her to do. Let her read. Let her build her map of what we are. A map is a
story, Rolk. And the best lies begin with true geography."

He did not take the shot. Instead, he began to count her steps as she rose
and walked — not toward the wreckage, but along the gorge's edge, her head
turning slowly, cataloguing. Twelve steps north. Pause. Eight steps east.
Pause. She was triangulating the *absence* of life, the places where his
people were hiding so well that they read as holes in the world.

Clever. Clean. And exactly the kind of cleverness that forgot a single
truth about Kalee:

The dead here outnumbered the living a thousand to one. And the dead,
unlike the living, could not be sensed at all.

By nightfall she had found nothing but ghosts, and Qymaen had learned
everything he needed: how she moved, how she doubted, and where — when the
moment came — she would not think to look.

## DAY 3: What the Dead Remember

On the third day it stopped raining, and Kalee showed its true face.

The lowlands turned to rust and amber under a sky the color of old brass.
Qymaen descended from the high ground alone, leaving Rolk with the rifles.
This part was not for an audience. This part was a conversation.

He found her at the cairn field.

She should not have been able to find it — the cairns were old, older than
the war, older than the Huk, stones stacked by hands that had never heard
the word *Republic*. But she stood among them with her lightsaber
unlit in her hand and her head bowed, and Qymaen understood, with a slow
cold certainty, that she had not come to hunt him at all.

"You came to ask them something," he said.

She turned. She did not startle. The green of her face had gone grey with
the dust of the lowlands, and her eyes were the steady yellow-amber of
someone walking very close to an edge.

"I came to return something," she said. Her voice was lower than he had
expected. Unhurried. "A long time ago, a Kaleesh came to my Temple. He
carried a child he could not feed, and he asked us to take it." She paused.
"We did not. We were... advised that the conflict here was internal. That
to intervene would be to take a side."

Qymaen said nothing. The Gravedancer in his skull had gone very quiet.

"The child died in the famine that followed." Kaelen Voss lowered her
head. "I have carried that not-intervention for nineteen years. I came
here to find where the child was buried, and to say what I should have
said then."

The wind moved across the cairn field, and the stones did not answer her,
because stones never do. But Qymaen — who had spent his whole life
learning the language of the dead — heard, underneath the wind, the shape
of what she was really saying.

She was asking to be forgiven. And she had come to the one place in the
galaxy where forgiveness did not live.

"You are standing on the child's grave," Qymaen said softly. "This cairn.
The third stone from the east."

She looked down. Her hand opened, and the lightsaber — unlit, useless —
fell into the dust.

And in that exact moment, the precise and terrible instant in which a
Jedi knelt unarmed over a grave she had helped to make, Qymaen jai Sheelal
understood what he was becoming, and could not bring himself, *yet*, to
stop.
